Introduction

Fraudulent credit card transactions are a growing concern for consumers and financial institutions alike. With the rise of online shopping and digital transactions, the risk of fraud has increased, resulting in millions of dollars in losses each year. Credit card fraud occurs through several methods including: skimming devices at ATMs or point-of-sale (POS) terminals, phishing scams, identity theft, and counterfeiting.

In skimming, a device is placed on an ATM or POS terminal to steal credit card information when the card is used. Phishing scams are when fraudsters send emails or texts posing as a reputable entity to trick individuals into revealing their credit card information. Identity theft occurs when a criminal obtains personal information and uses it to open credit card accounts or make unauthorized charges. Counterfeiting involves creating a fake credit card with a stolen or fabricated credit card number. In all cases, it is crucial for individuals to monitor their credit card statements and report any suspicious activity immediately.

The Fraud Detection System (FDS) can be seen as a binary classification problem that seeks to separate fraudulent from legitimate transactions. Generally, the three categories of prior learning strategies include supervised techniques, unsupervised techniques, and semi-supervised techniques.

  1. Supervised Techniques: These rely on the historical transactions with true labels, in which the true labels are collected either from the feedback of users or the investigation results on financial rules.
  2. Unsupervised Techinqiues: These do not require any true label. Instead, they establish various anomaly detectors with the assumption that the outliers are fraudulent.
  3. Semi-Supervised Techniques: These techniques are utilized to conduct fraud detection, which can be viewed as a combination of the Supervised and Unsupervised techniques. The methods belonging to this category adopt a large scale of unlabeled transactions with a few correctly labeled ones.

One recent example of a fraudulent transaction case is the Capital One data breach in 2019. A hacker gained unauthorized access to the personal information of over 100 million Capital One customers, including names, addresses, credit scores, and credit card information.
